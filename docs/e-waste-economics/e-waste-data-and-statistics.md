## Global E-Waste Generation and Projections

The volume of e-waste generated globally is increasing at an alarming rate. The primary driver is the growing consumption of electronic devices, coupled with shorter product lifecycles and limited repair options.

| Year | E-Waste Generated (Million Tonnes) | Growth Since 2010 |
|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 34 | - |
| 2022 | 62 | +82% |
| 2030 (Projected) | 82 | +141% |
*Source: The Global E-waste Monitor 2024 [1]*

This rapid growth far outpaces the development of recycling infrastructure, with the amount of e-waste generated annually expected to be five times greater than the capacity of formal recycling systems by 2030 [4].

## The Economic Value of Materials in E-Waste

E-waste is a significant reservoir of valuable and critical raw materials. The failure to recycle these materials represents a substantial economic loss.

### Value of Embedded Metals (2022)

|---|---|
| Copper | $19 Billion |

| Iron | $16 Billion |
| **Total (All Metals)** | **$91 Billion** |
*Source: The Global E-waste Monitor 2024 [1]*

### Urban Mining Potential

The concentration of valuable metals in e-waste is often significantly higher than in virgin ores, making "urban mining" an economically attractive proposition.

- A tonne of e-waste can contain **40 to 800 times more gold** than a tonne of gold ore [2].
- Only **1% of the demand for rare earth elements** is currently met through e-waste recycling [1].

## The Cost of Improper Disposal

The economic impact of e-waste is not limited to the loss of valuable materials. The costs associated with improper disposal, including environmental damage and public health impacts, are immense.

### Externalized Costs (2022)

| Cost Category | Estimated Annual Cost (USD) |
|---|---|
| Greenhouse Gas Emissions | $36 Billion |
| Mercury Exposure | $22 Billion |
| Plastic Waste Pollution | $19 Billion |
| Lead Contamination | ~$1 Billion |
| **Total** | **$78 Billion** |
*Source: Kearney, 2025 [4]*

These figures highlight the significant economic burden that improper e-waste management places on society.

## Job Creation and Market Growth

The transition to a circular economy for electronics is a significant driver of employment and economic growth.

- **6.32 million jobs** are currently supported by the global e-waste value chain [7].
- The e-waste recycling market is projected to grow from **$40.27 billion in 2025 to $44.84 billion in 2026** [8].
- Over **80% of e-waste related jobs** are in lower-middle and upper-middle-income countries, highlighting the sector's importance for livelihoods in developing economies [7].
